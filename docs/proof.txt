## 1) Risk-aware portfolio construction

Assumption
- Portfolio generation should optimize for risk-adjusted return instead of raw return alone.

Why this method is chosen
- The app centers on return, drawdown, Sharpe/Sortino-style metrics, and mandate-driven allocation in Generate and Backtest.
- The backend is structured around constrained portfolio construction and replay rather than execution routing.

Why the specific values are used
- The current branch keeps explicit risk-mode universe lists and target sector weights so the demo remains deterministic and inspectable.
- Those values are control parameters for the allocator, not arbitrary UI labels.

Evidence in the directory
- The portfolio orchestration lives in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L55) and is invoked by the portfolio/backtest routes.
- The current UI flow for generation and replay is wired in [src/App.tsx](src/App.tsx) and the portfolio/backtest components.

Research citations
- Markowitz, H. (1952). *Portfolio Selection*. Journal of Finance, 7(1), 77-91. Foundational mean-variance framework for risk-adjusted portfolio construction.
- Ledoit, O., & Wolf, M. (2004). *A Well-Conditioned Estimator for Large-Dimensional Covariance Matrices*. Journal of Multivariate Analysis, 88(2), 365-411. Supports stable covariance estimation for constrained allocation.
- Zhang, Z., Zohren, S., & Roberts, S. (2021). *Deep Learning for Portfolio Optimization*. arXiv:2005.13665. Supports friction-aware and risk-aware portfolio construction with modern ML signals.

## 2) Multi-signal runtime behavior

Assumption
- A multi-signal path is preferable to a single-signal path when local artifacts are available.

Why this method is chosen
- The runtime distinguishes between `full_ensemble`, `degraded_ensemble`, and `rules_only` instead of pretending every artifact is always present.
- This keeps the demo honest: core functionality continues when optional models are missing.

Why the specific values are used
- The manifest defaults use component weights of `0.50` for LightGBM, `0.25` for LSTM, `0.15` for GNN, and a `1.5` death-risk penalty.
- Those numbers are a pragmatic heuristic: LightGBM is the core signal, sequence/graph models are supplemental, and death risk is a negative adjustment rather than a positive alpha source.

Evidence in the directory
- [apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py](apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py) computes component readiness, runtime mode, and fallback notes at startup and on demand.
- [apps/api/app/services/ensemble_scorer.py](apps/api/app/services/ensemble_scorer.py) combines LightGBM, LSTM, GNN, and death-risk into a single score path.

Research citations
- Dietterich, T. G. (2000). *Ensemble Methods in Machine Learning*. Multiple Classifier Systems. Supports combining multiple weak/heterogeneous predictors for stronger and more robust performance.
- Zhou, Z.-H. (2012). *Ensemble Methods: Foundations and Algorithms*. CRC Press. Supports weighted multi-model aggregation when component strengths differ.

## 3) Realistic backtest frictions

Assumption
- Backtests should include taxes, brokerage, stamp duty, GST, and slippage instead of only raw price returns.

Why this method is chosen
- The app is meant to show an honest Indian-market research experience.
- Friction-aware backtests prevent the UI from overstating performance.

Why the specific values are used
- The friction and tax values are not invented in the UI; they come from the market-rule schedule and are wired into the historical replay path.
- The exact windows and cost assumptions are deterministic so the same input produces the same replay outcome.

Evidence in the directory
- `db_quant_engine.py` is the backtest orchestrator and uses fee and tax schedules from the market-rule services.
- The Backtest UI surfaces tax liability, transaction costs, and equity-curve comparison.

Research citations
- Novy-Marx, R., & Velikov, M. (2016). *A Taxonomy of Anomalies and Their Trading Costs*. Review of Financial Studies, 29(1), 104-147. Shows that implementation frictions materially affect realizable returns.
- Bailey, D. H., Borwein, J. M., Lopez de Prado, M., & Zhu, Q. J. (2014). *The Probability of Backtest Overfitting*. Journal of Computational Finance, 20(4), 39-69. Supports conservative, friction-aware backtest design.

## 4) Proxy benchmarks for local demo research

Assumption
- Proxy benchmarks are acceptable when official historical constituent reconstruction is not fully available.

Why this method is chosen
- The Compare tab needs a reproducible local benchmark view for the demo.
- The branch favors labeled proxy benchmarks rather than silent approximation.

Why the specific values are used
- The chosen benchmark set balances usefulness and simplicity: an AI strategy row, an index proxy row, factor-style rows, and an AMC-style blended row.
- This gives the user a compact comparison surface without pretending to be a licensed benchmark feed.

Evidence in the directory
- `BENCHMARK_UNIVERSES` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L67) defines the comparison set used by the backend.
- The Compare tab uses the backend benchmark summary and shows proxy metadata and limitations.

Research citations
- Roll, R. (1992). *A Mean/Variance Analysis of Tracking Error*. Journal of Portfolio Management, 18(4), 13-22. Supports evaluating benchmark proxies using tracking-error framing.
- Beasley, J. E., Meade, N., & Chang, T.-J. (2003). *An Evolutionary Heuristic for the Index Tracking Problem*. European Journal of Operational Research, 148(3), 621-643. Supports practical proxy/replication approaches when full benchmark reconstruction is constrained.

## 5) Groq as a secondary explanation layer

Assumption
- Explanation and chat are useful, but they must remain secondary to the quant path.

Why this method is chosen
- The quant workflow must keep functioning when Groq is unavailable.
- Groq therefore lives behind explanation/chat routes rather than in the portfolio math path.

Why the specific values are used
- The Groq API key is optional in the runtime configuration, and the UI presents Groq as a capability rather than a requirement.
- That choice avoids coupling core demo availability to a single external key or network dependency.

Evidence in the directory

- The current frontend no longer includes the AI chat widget.

Research citations

## 6) Explicit fallback modes

Assumption
- It is better to show the current runtime state explicitly than to fail silently.

Why this method is chosen
- The UI can report whether the system is running as `full_ensemble`, `degraded_ensemble`, or `rules_only`.
- This avoids misleading the user about what model path is actually active.

Why the specific values are used
- The runtime modes are intentionally coarse and legible: they capture whether the core LightGBM path is present and whether the non-core signals are available.

Evidence in the directory
- `GET /api/v1/models/current` is the source of truth for readiness.
- The UI fetches that runtime status and shows it in the generation/backtest surfaces.

Research citations
- Avizienis, A., Laprie, J.-C., Randell, B., & Landwehr, C. (2004). *Basic Concepts and Taxonomy of Dependable and Secure Computing*. IEEE TDSC, 1(1), 11-33. Supports explicit fault/degradation state modeling.
- Vogels, W. (2009). *Eventually Consistent*. Communications of the ACM, 52(1), 40-44. Supports transparent trade-offs and degraded operation in distributed systems.

## 8) API/UI contract parity

Assumption

Why this method is chosen
- The UI tabs share one backend API layer instead of each calling fetch independently.

Why the specific values are used
- The current tab set and route set are small enough that one adapter is simpler and more reliable than multiple divergent request implementations.

Evidence in the directory

## 10) Value-by-value rationale

This section explains the main assumed constants that appear in the current directory.

### Backtest defaults

- `startDate: '2025-01-01'` and `endDate: '2025-12-31'` in [src/components/BacktestTab.tsx](src/components/BacktestTab.tsx#L15) are a full-year default window so the demo has enough history to show regime changes, drawdowns, and recovery without forcing the user to configure dates first.
- `stopLossPct: 0.15` in [src/components/BacktestTab.tsx](src/components/BacktestTab.tsx#L17) means a 15% stop-loss. This is high enough to avoid exiting on ordinary day-to-day noise, but low enough to cut a position before a larger trend failure becomes unrecoverable in a demo backtest.
- `takeProfitPct: 0.40` in [src/components/BacktestTab.tsx](src/components/BacktestTab.tsx#L18) means a 40% profit target. The value is intentionally wider than the stop so the replay can let strong momentum names run while still showing a disciplined exit rule.
- `rebalanceFreq: 'Quarterly'` in [src/components/BacktestTab.tsx](src/components/BacktestTab.tsx#L19) balances turnover against responsiveness. Monthly would make the demo noisier and more cost-heavy; annual would be too stale for a research UI.

### Generate / mandate defaults

- `investment_horizon_weeks: '4-8'` in [src/components/GenerateTab.tsx](src/components/GenerateTab.tsx#L59) is a mid-term horizon that matches a retail research workflow without pretending to be intraday or multi-year pension logic.
- `max_portfolio_drawdown_pct: 12` in [src/components/GenerateTab.tsx](src/components/GenerateTab.tsx#L60) is a moderate tolerance that fits the `balanced` default and keeps the allocator from chasing high-volatility names too aggressively.
- `max_position_size_pct: 12.5` in [src/components/GenerateTab.tsx](src/components/GenerateTab.tsx#L61) keeps single-name concentration under control while still allowing a small set of high-conviction holdings to matter.
- `preferred_num_positions: 10` in [src/components/GenerateTab.tsx](src/components/GenerateTab.tsx#L62) is a practical compromise between diversification and readability. Fewer names would over-concentrate the book; many more would make the demo noisy and dilute the model signal.
- `risk_attitude: 'balanced'` in [src/components/GenerateTab.tsx](src/components/GenerateTab.tsx#L66) is the safest default for a demo because it is neither overly conservative nor overly aggressive.

### Runtime and ensemble defaults

- `component_weights: { lightgbm: 0.50, lstm: 0.25, gnn: 0.15 }` in [apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py](apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py#L73) gives the strongest weight to the tabular model because it is the most direct and inspectable local signal, while the sequence/graph models act as supplements.
- `death_risk_penalty: 1.5` in [apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py](apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py#L74) makes the crash-risk term a meaningful negative adjustment without fully overwhelming the expected-return model.
- `prediction_horizon_days: 21` in [apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py](apps/api/app/services/model_runtime.py#L80) aligns with a roughly one-month trading horizon and is consistent across the component metadata and frontend displays.
- In `LIGHTGBM_HYBRID` generation, selected names are stamped with `expected_return_source = LIGHTGBM`, `model_version = lightgbm_v1`, and `prediction_horizon_days = 21` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py). This keeps attribution explicit by showing when ML values are actually used in generated outputs.

### Portfolio engine defaults

- `RISK_MODE_UNIVERSES` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L55) is intentionally small and curated so the demo stays reproducible and the allocator has a known liquid universe to work with.
- `candidate_count: 10` for `ULTRA_LOW` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L134) keeps the ultra-conservative portfolio sparse and cash-like.
- `candidate_count: 12` for `MODERATE` and `HIGH` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L135) gives enough breadth to diversify while keeping the UI manageable.
- `max_weight: 0.35`, `0.16`, and `0.12` for the three risk modes in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L134) through [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L136) progressively tighten concentration as the mandate becomes more aggressive or more conservative.
- `sector_cap: 0.42`, `0.28`, and `0.24` in the same block prevent any single sector from dominating the portfolio, which is especially important in a small demo universe.
- `risk_aversion: 18.0`, `8.5`, and `4.25` reflect a deliberately stepped preference curve: very defensive, balanced, and growth-oriented mandates should not rank candidates the same way.
- `shrinkage: 0.55`, `0.40`, and `0.30` keep covariance estimation stable on limited local history. More defensive modes use heavier shrinkage because they should prefer robustness over sensitivity.
- `turnover_penalty: 0.10`, `0.06`, and `0.04` limit needless churn. Lower penalty values are allowed in higher-risk modes because those mandates tolerate more activity.
- `drift_threshold: 0.06`, `0.04`, and `0.03` define when a rebalance is worth acting on. The threshold is looser for ultra-low turnover portfolios and tighter for more active ones.
- `min_trade_weight: 0.03`, `0.025`, and `0.02` avoid tiny, mostly cosmetic trades that would clutter the replay with noise.
- `cooldown_days: 5`, `7`, and `10` slow repeated churn after a rebalance so the demo looks like a managed portfolio instead of a hyperactive trading bot.

### Benchmark defaults

- `BENCHMARK_UNIVERSES` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L67) uses a small set of labeled research proxies because the branch does not yet reconstruct licensed benchmark constituents.
- The factor portfolios use `count=12` and the AMC-style sleeve uses `count=15` in [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L739) through [apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py](apps/api/app/services/db_quant_engine.py#L741) so the benchmark views stay diversified but not overly crowded.
- The benchmark sector caps of `0.25` and `0.22` in the same lines keep the proxy comparisons from collapsing into one sector even when a factor score is concentrated.

### Local dev CORS defaults

- `http://localhost:3000`, `http://127.0.0.1:3000`, `http://localhost:3001`, `http://127.0.0.1:3001`, `http://localhost:4173`, `http://127.0.0.1:4173`, `http://localhost:5173`, and `http://127.0.0.1:5173` in [apps/api/app/core/config.py](apps/api/app/core/config.py#L6) through [apps/api/app/core/config.py](apps/api/app/core/config.py#L14) cover the common local and smoke-test frontends used in this repository.
- `cors_origin_regex` in [apps/api/app/core/config.py](apps/api/app/core/config.py#L16) is there so future localhost ports still work without requiring a code change for every new dev port.

### Why these values are not arbitrary

- They trade off realism, determinism, and demo usability.
- They were chosen to keep the system explainable in a local directory snapshot, not to maximize a hidden live-market benchmark.
- Where a value is a heuristic, the code and UI expose it as a heuristic instead of pretending it is a proven constant.
